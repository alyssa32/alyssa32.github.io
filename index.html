Artist Statement

My artistic practice explores the complex relationship between nature, technology, and human influence. Through a combination of 3D modeling, drawing, and mixed media, I examine how perception shapes our understanding of the environment and challenge the ways we interact with the natural world. I am particularly interested in the subtle imprints we leave behind—whether physical, digital, or sensory. My work often reflects on how nature is framed, controlled, or altered, inviting viewers to reconsider what is seen, heard, and felt. I aim to visualize the hidden forces at play—whether through the distortion of landscapes, the lasting presence of sound, or the tension between control and resistance. Rather than presenting nature as something distant or idealized, I use art to highlight its dynamic, evolving state, shaped by both human and non-human forces. My work serves as a bridge between reality and perception, encouraging a deeper awareness of the environment and our place within it.

My Portfolio

3D Models

Framed Reality

My artwork explores the contrast between technology and nature in shaping our perception of reality. I have created two televisions, each representing a different way of experiencing the world. The first is a retro television, made of processed wood, plastic, and metal. It sits alone in a stark, empty room—an isolated object, much like how media can sometimes disconnect us from reality. Its glossy screen reflects faint images of a forest, offering only a fragmented, controlled version of nature, limiting what the viewer sees. In contrast, the second television is crafted entirely from raw materials—wood, stone, and bark. Its antenna is a natural wooden branch, its knobs are rocks, and its most striking feature is the absence of a screen. Without a barrier between the viewer and their surroundings, this piece invites them to engage directly with the world—to see, feel, and experience reality without the filter of media. By removing the screen, it opens up the senses, allowing for a personal, unfiltered perception of nature rather than a mediated one.

Seeing Sound

This 3D artwork explores the idea that sound never truly disappears—it only fades beyond human perception. According to the law of conservation of energy, sound waves do not vanish, they simply lose energy over time, continuing to exist in its environment. The scene presents a seemingly natural landscape, however the space appears warped and shifted near the base of the trees, visualizing a build-up of lingering sound waves throughout history. This distortion is not meant to be seen as sound pollution in a negative sense, but rather as a reminder of the unseen energies from our world’s past.

Weighted Silence

Saying “no” when people ask something of me, is one of the main things I struggle with. I end up taking on so many tasks, that I sacrifice my healthy eating habits, sleep, and a calm state of mind. The concept behind my work is not just a visual representation of how I feel when I undergo stress, the creature is my stress. The environment is familiar to me, as it is where I spend most of my time working catching up on projects until night. The mounds of work I have pilled up is why she has additional fingers and hands growing out of her arms. I imagine my creature would make minimal noise walking around and in general, just like how I work in silence. If you were to reach inside my mind and somehow visualize my stress, I would think this creature would be found inside.

Drawings



Rooted in Silence

Exploring the connection between environmental destruction and the oppression of women, the sepia coloured ink drawing shows a dead tree with seven female figures trapped within its trunk, their bodies tangled in the wood. Some branches take the shape of hands, reaching outward. The tree stands alone in an empty space, symbolizing loss and isolation. This piece is inspired by ecofeminism, which sees a link between the way nature and women are often controlled, used, and overlooked. Just as forests are cut down for resources, women—especially those in marginalized communities—often suffer the most from environmental harm while having little say in the decisions that cause it. The figures in the tree represent both pain and strength, showing how women and nature continue to exist despite exploitation.

The Last Fight

"The Last Fight" critiques the cruelty of rodeo practices and the struggle for freedom. The horse is drawn using pastels, full of life and movement, while the cowboys are in black and white pencil, making them feel cold and detached. This contrast highlights the imbalance between the horse and its faceless captors. This piece invites viewers to consider the ethics of rodeo culture and the power struggle between human control and an animal’s right to freedom.

The Egg

This still-life drawing was made with white pastel and pencil on grey paper. Focusing this light study drawing not only on the direct light hitting the egg, but also the light reflecting off the table. Allowing the viewer to appreciate the subtle details of such a subtle object.

Graphic Design

Cafe Agoump

Using Procreate, I designed this logo for Agoump Cafe. This cafe was run by our scouts team in order to raise money for our humanitarian trip to Armenia this summer of 2023. I kept the design of the mug simple to allow the coffee colours to stand out, which were inspired by the Armenian flag. The "o" is replaced by Armenia's eternity sign which symbolizes the identity of it's nation.
